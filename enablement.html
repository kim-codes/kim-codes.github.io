Why Great Enablement Starts Long Before Training

Enablement isn't about teaching people what to do. It's about understanding what prevents them from succeeding in the first place.

Most organizations jump straight to creating content. But the real work starts with understanding the problem.

Every enablement effort is really an attempt to close the gap between the current state and the desired one.

Enablement shouldn't be seen as filling knowledge gaps. It's about identifying the friction that prevents people from succeeding and helping remove it.

Where are things breaking down?

Before building content, you first need to understand where alignment is breaking down. Sometimes that's knowledge. More often it's messaging, process, priorities, or even the product itself.

If you solve the wrong problem, even the best training won't change the outcome.

1. Start with the problem, not the content.

It's tempting to jump straight into creating a course, a slide deck, or a playbook. Creating content feels like progress.

But content is usually the last step, not the first. The first step is listening.

Talk to sellers, product managers, customer success, and customers. Understand what's actually getting in the way.

2. Design for behavior, not consumption.

Success isn't someone finishing a course. The goal is changing how someone approaches their work.

Can they explain the value of a product more clearly? Handle difficult questions with confidence?

If the behavior doesn't change, it doesn't matter how polished the content was.

3. Measure outcomes, not activity.

Attendance and completion rates are easy to report. But they are rarely the metrics that matter.

I care more about whether conversations improve. Is onboarding becoming faster? Do teams feel more confident in front of customers?

Those are harder to measure, but they're much closer to the reason enablement exists in the first place.

The best enablement often looks invisible.

It doesn't feel like enablement. It feels like clarity.

When people have the right context, the right messaging, and confidence in what they're communicating, the training itself fades into the background.

They simply do their jobs better.
