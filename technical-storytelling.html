How I Turn Technical Features into Stories

A feature isn't valuable because it exists. It's valuable because someone understands why it matters.

My Approach

Simplify Understanding

Most products don't have a feature problem. They have a communication problem.

My job isn't to simplify technology. It's to simplify understanding.

Whenever I'm learning a new feature, I usually ask:

  1. What frustrating thing becomes easier?
  2. Who feels that frustration the most?
  3. What changes after someone adopts it?
  4. Can I explain it to someone outside of engineering?

Lessons

Things I've Learned Explaining Technology

Developers don't buy features.

They buy confidence.

Documentation is marketing.

Every tutorial teaches more than syntax—it communicates what your product values.

Good demos tell stories.

The best demos help someone imagine solving a real problem.

Customer questions are product feedback.

If the same question keeps coming up, the messaging probably deserves another look.

Starting Somewhere New

I'd Start by Listening

Before changing messaging I'd want to understand:

  • What developers love.
  • What consistently confuses them.
  • What Sales hears every week.
  • What Product wishes customers understood better.

Only then would I start changing the story.

Still figuring it out.

I don't think there's a perfect formula for great product marketing. Every product, every team, and every customer is different. These are simply the principles I keep coming back to because they've served me well across developer relations, open source, enablement, and product marketing.
